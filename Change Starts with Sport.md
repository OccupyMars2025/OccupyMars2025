
Here's a rundown of various types of flips you might come across in gymnastics, acrobatics, martial arts, or parkour:

Simple Flips:
Backflip (or Back Tuck): A backward rotation where the body rotates around its horizontal axis, finishing in a standing position.
Front Flip (or Front Tuck): A forward rotation where the body flips forward, landing on the feet.

Variations Based on Direction and Body Position:
Layout: A flip performed with the body straight and extended rather than tucked.
Back Layout: A backflip with the body straight.
Front Layout: A front flip with the body straight.
Pike: The body is bent at the waist with legs straight.
Back Pike: A backflip where the legs are straight and together, bent at the waist.
Front Pike: Similar but forward.
Tuck: The body is folded at the knees, with the thighs close to the chest.
Back Tuck: The most common form of backflip, with the body in a tight tuck position.
Front Tuck: Similar, but flipping forward.

Multiple Rotations:
Double Backflip: Two backward rotations before landing.
Double Front Flip: Two forward rotations.
Triple Backflip: Three backward rotations, very advanced.
Triple Front Flip: Three forward rotations, also very advanced.

Twisting Flips:
Half Twist: Any flip with a 180-degree twist.
Half Twist Backflip: Backflip with a half turn.
Half Twist Front Flip: Front flip with a half turn.
Full Twist: Any flip with a 360-degree twist.
Full Twist Backflip: Backflip with a full turn.
Full Twist Front Flip: Front flip with a full turn.
Double Full (or Double Twist): Two full rotations in the air.
Triple Twist: Three full rotations in the air, extremely difficult.

Aerial Flips:
Aerial (Cartwheel Without Hands): A side flip where the body is parallel to the ground, similar to a cartwheel but without hand support.
Side Somersault: Essentially an aerial but with more rotation.

Specialized Flips:
Gainers: A backflip where the jumper runs forward but flips backward, often used off platforms or springboards.
Webster: A backflip with a 180-degree twist, typically starting from one foot.
Corkscrew: A flip that combines forward rotation with a twist, often seen in wakeboarding or snowboarding.
Barani Flip: A front flip with a half twist (180 degrees), named after its creator, Uwe Barani.
Rudolph: A front flip with a full twist, ending in a back position.

Parkour and Freerunning Specific:
Palm Flip: A flip where the hands are used to push off from a wall or rail.
Wall Flip: Flip off a wall, often using the hands for momentum.

This list isn't exhaustive, as athletes and performers often invent new variations or combine moves in unique ways. Each discipline might have its own terminology or slight variations on these flips. Remember, performing these flips requires significant training, practice, and safety measures to avoid injury.

Each route offers unique challenges but also scenic views. The Qinghai-Tibet Highway is noted for being relatively easier for cyclists as it has less elevation change and is mostly paved.
Altitude and Acclimatization: One of the biggest challenges is the altitude. Lhasa itself is at an elevation of approximately 3,650 meters above sea level. Cyclists must consider acclimatization to avoid altitude sickness, especially when approaching the Tibetan Plateau. Some routes allow for gradual ascent, which can help with acclimatization, but the change from sea level in Shanghai to high altitudes requires careful planning.
Physical Demand and Preparation: This journey would require exceptional physical fitness and endurance. Cyclists would need to prepare for the diverse climate conditions, from the humid and warm conditions of Shanghai to the cold and potentially harsh weather of the plateau. Logistics: Traveling through multiple provinces, cyclists would need to manage logistics like food, water, accommodation, and potential mechanical issues with their bikes. Permits and Restrictions: For international travelers, entering Tibet requires a Tibet Travel Permit, which must be arranged through a travel agency. This adds an administrative layer to the preparation for such a trip.
Real-World Examples: There are documented cases of individuals completing this journey, like the example of a single father and his daughter cycling from Guangdong to Lhasa, which, while not from Shanghai, demonstrates that such a long-distance cycle is achievable with determination and proper preparation.
